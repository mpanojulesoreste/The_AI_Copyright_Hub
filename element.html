Copyrights 101: Protecting Creativity in the Digital Age

What is Copyright?

Copyright is a widely used legal concept that allows creators of intellectual property to receive credit and compensation for their ideas. These copyrights can be for different types of intellectual property from an app, to music, to a new invention. They usually last for certain periods of time from 50-70 or 120 years depending on the material.
 

Why is Copyright important?

Copyright is an extremely important concept as it protects creators and their intellectual property. Without it, it is possible for people to steal ideas from others and claim their products as their own, reaping potential benefits that rightfully go to the original creator. Creativity would also be stifled without the incentive that copyright provides. Without any benefit to create your own unique idea such as monetization and royalties, there would be a significant decrease in new productions as there would be no reward for the work it takes to produce new, unique products.
 

Historical Background of Copyrights

The origins of copyright can be dated back to ancient civilizations, where those in power had control over the creation and distribution of texts. However, more technical terms of copyright were solidified in more modern societies, like the British Statute of Anne, in which Publishers of books were granted 14 years of legal protection. The U.S Constitution adopted a similar law, granting citizens 14 years of legal protection of intellectual property, with ability to renew after certain periods of time. The first ever U.S court case regarding copyright was in 1834 with the Wheaton vs Peters case. Since then, there have been many amendments to the copyright laws within the U.S, with notable changes such as the Copyright Act of 1909 which granted protection to published works filed under copyright, while subjecting published and un-copyrighted work to the “public domain”. The Copyright Act of 1976 was the next major addition to the field, extending the protection of copyright to both published and unpublished works in any tangible form. With the Sonny Bono Copyright Term Extension Act, copyright could span the life of the author/creator plus 70 years, or 120 years from the creation of the product. Now, in the modern digital age, copyright protection has taken a more complicated turn. In 2014, the Copyright Office amended their Office Practices for only the third time in history, making over 1,200 pages of rules and regulations. As well, in 2018, a Music Modernization Act was passed regarding the protection of music and sound recordings both pre and post 1972 and focused on artist royalties.

Now, with the rise of artificial intelligence, the need for copyright is possibly at its highest demand, but how can copyright and AI coexist with so many limitations and overlaps?
